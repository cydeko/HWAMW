## Description
Varying temporal distance to your experience—telling the same story as if from yesterday, last year, ten years ago, or looking back from your deathbed. This technique reveals how meaning shifts with temporal perspective: immediate experiences feel different when narrated from the recent past, distant past, present moment, or imagined future vantage points.

## Prompts
1. Imagine you're in the distant past, long before this experience happened. If you could see it coming, what would you have thought it would mean or teach you?
2. Think about the time shortly after this experience ended. What did it mean to you then, when it was still fresh and recent?
3. From your perspective right now, in this present moment, what does this experience mean to you?
4. Now imagine yourself a few years from now, looking back at this experience. What do you think you'll see or understand about it then?
5. Finally, imagine yourself at the end of your life, looking back across all your years. From that vantage point, what role does this experience play in the story of your life?

## Section Structure
Each section should feel self-contained — landing somewhere emotionally — while making the next feel necessary. The three sections are three distinct temporal vantage points; the same events should feel genuinely different from each distance.

- **Section 1 — The Recent Past**: The experience as it felt shortly after it ended — still raw, still close, meaning not yet settled. The protagonist is inside it, trying to make sense of it in real time. Ends with the unresolved feeling of something not yet understood.
- **Section 2 — The Present**: The same experience from where the protagonist stands now. What time has clarified, what it has complicated. The distance is real but the events are still part of who they are. Ends with the story neither closed nor open — held.
- **Section 3 — The Far Future**: Looking back from the end of a life. The experience has found its place in the whole. What role it played, what it gave, what it cost. Closes with the long view: this moment as one thread in something larger.
