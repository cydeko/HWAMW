## Description
Using physical objects or possessions as symbolic anchors that crystallize the meaning of life events or periods. This technique grounds abstract experiences in concrete things—a piece of jewelry, a photograph, a childhood toy—allowing material culture to serve as a mnemonic device for understanding your narrative.

## Prompts
1. What's an object that reminds you of this experience whenever you see it? What does it represent?
2. What's an object that played a key role in this experience but you've since forgotten, lost, or misplaced?
3. If you could choose one object to symbolize what you learned or how you changed, what would it be?
4. What object captures the emotional core of this experience—how it felt rather than what happened?
5. Looking at these objects together, what do they reveal about the deeper meaning of this experience?

## Section Structure
Each section should feel self-contained — landing somewhere emotionally — while making the next feel necessary. Objects anchor each section; they should feel specific and real, not generic symbols.

- **Section 1 — The Object That Remains**: An object the protagonist still has — something that carries the memory of the experience whenever encountered. The section moves between the object and what it holds. Ends with the weight of what is still present, still unresolved.
- **Section 2 — The Object That Was Lost**: Something that played a role in the experience but is gone — misplaced, forgotten, left behind. Its absence is its own kind of presence. Ends with what the loss of the object reveals about what the experience cost.
- **Section 3 — The Object That Symbolises Change**: An object — real or imagined — that captures what was learned or how the protagonist changed. May not exist yet; may need to be found or made. Closes with what the object means: the experience distilled into something you could hold in your hands.
