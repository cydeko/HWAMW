## Description
Retelling your experience from multiple perspectives—yourself, another person involved, an outside observer—to reveal dimensions invisible from any single viewpoint. This technique shows how experiences are about more than just a sole protagonist, and how their meanings can shift based on who is viewing them.

## Prompts
1. From your own perspective at the time, what did this experience mean to you? What were you trying to achieve or understand?
2. Think of someone else who was involved in or affected by this experience. How might they have perceived what was happening? What were their needs, concerns, or interpretations?
3. What might that person have noticed about you or the situation that you couldn't see yourself?
4. Now imagine a neutral observer watching this experience unfold—someone with no stake in the outcome. What would stand out to them as significant or meaningful?
5. What would this outside observer understand about the experience that neither you nor the other person involved could fully see?

## Section Structure
Each section should feel self-contained — landing somewhere emotionally — while making the next feel necessary. The three sections are three distinct consciousnesses observing the same events; each one should feel like a genuinely different mind.

- **Section 1 — The Protagonist's View**: The experience as the protagonist lived it from the inside — their intentions, their interpretation, what they were reaching for. Rendered with interiority and partial vision. Ends with something unresolved or unseen.
- **Section 2 — The Other's View**: The same experience through the eyes of another key person — their needs, fears, and interpretations. What they noticed that the protagonist couldn't. This section should make the reader feel the protagonist is being seen from outside for the first time. Ends with something the protagonist didn't know they had revealed.
- **Section 3 — The Observer's View**: A neutral witness with no stake in the outcome. They see both people clearly, understand what neither fully could. Closes with an insight that belongs to no one inside the story — something that only becomes visible from outside.
