## Description
Emphasizing the embodied, visceral dimensions of experience through rich sensory description—sights, sounds, smells, textures, physical sensations. This technique moves meaning from abstract reflection into lived, bodily experience, potentially revealing how your story feels in your body rather than just how it sounds in your mind.

## Prompts
1. What did you see during this experience? What visual details stand out in your memory?
2. What sounds do you remember—voices, music, ambient noise, or silence?
3. Were there any particular smells or tastes associated with this experience?
4. What physical sensations or textures do you recall—what did things feel like to touch, or how did your body feel?
5. Taking all these sensory details together, how do they capture the essence of what this experience meant to you?

## Section Structure
Each section should feel self-contained — landing somewhere emotionally — while making the next feel necessary. Each section moves through a different register of sensory experience; together they build a complete embodied portrait.

- **Section 1 — What Was Seen and Heard**: The visual and auditory texture of the experience — light, colour, faces, voices, ambient sound, silence. Rendered with precision. Ends with an image or sound that carries more weight than it should — a detail that knows something.
- **Section 2 — What Was Smelled, Tasted, Touched**: The more intimate senses — smell, taste, the feel of surfaces, the temperature of the air. These are the senses most tied to memory and emotion. Ends with a physical sensation that encodes the emotional core of the experience.
- **Section 3 — What the Body Held**: How the experience lived in the body — tension, relief, exhaustion, the physical shape of feeling. Not emotion described abstractly but emotion felt as sensation. Closes with the body as a record: all of it still there, held in the muscles and the breath.
